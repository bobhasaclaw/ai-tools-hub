# ChatGPT Article Rewrite Brief

Last reviewed: March 26, 2026

## Sitewide Rewrite Framework for Future AI Tools Hub Articles

### 1. Start with decision support, not feature dumping
Each article should help a reader answer: what is this tool, who is it for, what does it do well, where does it fall short, and when should someone choose an alternative instead.

### 2. Separate stable facts from fast-changing details
Keep the core product explanation evergreen, but date-stamp pricing, model availability, and feature rollouts. If a detail changes often, say so plainly rather than pretending it is fixed.

### 3. Use a repeatable article structure
Recommended section order:

1. Overview
2. Who it is for
3. Key features
4. Pricing
5. Real-world use cases
6. Strengths and weaknesses
7. Alternatives
8. Verdict
9. FAQ

### 4. Write like an editor, not a fan
Tone should be professional, educational, and specific. Avoid hype, inflated claims, and vague praise such as "revolutionary" or "game-changing" unless there is evidence.

### 5. Include a reality-check section
Every article should explain where the tool can mislead, overpromise, or require verification. Readers trust review content more when limitations are explicit.

### 6. Compare by fit, not by winner-takes-all rankings
Most AI tools overlap. Comparisons should explain which user, workflow, or ecosystem each tool fits best rather than claiming one tool is universally best.

### 7. Make the article easy to convert to HTML
Use clean markdown headings, short paragraphs, flat lists, and tables only where they improve scanning. Avoid layout-dependent formatting.

## Target Reader

- General readers comparing AI assistants
- Solo professionals and creators
- Students and researchers who need a capable everyday assistant
- Small teams evaluating whether ChatGPT Business is worth considering
- Readers who want practical guidance, not benchmarks or fan commentary

## Core Claims the Article Should Support

- ChatGPT remains one of the strongest general-purpose AI assistants because it combines writing help, coding help, web research, data analysis, image handling, voice, and agent-style workflows in one product.
- The free tier is useful for casual experimentation, but the paid tiers matter most for heavier daily use, higher limits, and premium tools.
- Plus is the practical upgrade for regular individual use; Pro is mainly for power users who push limits or want broader premium access.
- Business and Enterprise matter less for casual buyers and more for privacy, administration, collaboration, and governance.
- ChatGPT is not the best tool for every specialized job: citation-heavy research, deep Google integration, deep Microsoft integration, or some long-form writing workflows may point readers to alternatives.

## Fact Guardrails

- Pricing, plan features, model names, and usage limits change frequently. Use "as of March 26, 2026" language where exact current details are included.
- Do not imply that a ChatGPT subscription includes OpenAI API credits. It does not.
- Do not promise perfect accuracy or reliable sourcing. Emphasize verification for important work.
- Distinguish personal-plan data controls from business-plan privacy defaults.
- Treat model names as secondary detail. User value matters more than memorizing the latest picker labels.

## Proposed Outline

### 1. Overview

- Explain what ChatGPT is now: not just a chatbot, but a general AI assistant for text, voice, images, files, web search, and more advanced task workflows.
- Summarize the main editorial verdict in two to three paragraphs.
- Include a short note that OpenAI updates plan features and models frequently.

### 2. Who ChatGPT Is For

- Best for: general knowledge workers, creators, students, freelancers, analysts, PMs, marketers, and users who want one versatile AI product.
- Less ideal for: users needing guaranteed factual reliability, strict citations every time, or deep productivity-suite-native integration from Google or Microsoft.

- Multimodal chat: text, image, voice, file uploads
- Web search and deep research
- Data analysis and charts
- Projects for long-running work
- GPTs for custom assistants

### 4. Pricing

- Free: useful entry point
- Plus: $20/month as of March 26, 2026
- Pro: $200/month as of March 26, 2026
- Business: $25/user/month annually or $30/user/month monthly, as listed in current OpenAI help materials

- Mention that OpenAI also offers ChatGPT Go in some countries, but it is not broadly relevant to a typical U.S. buyer

- Writing and editing
- Research and summarization

## Research Notes for the Draft

### Official product positioning

- ChatGPT overview page positions the product around productivity, coding, learning, web search, data analysis, images, voice, and agent-assisted work.
- Projects are now a meaningful part of the product story, which helps present ChatGPT as a workspace and not just a chat box.
- GPTs remain important because they let users create or use task-specific assistants without writing code.

### Pricing and plan framing

- Plus is the main consumer paid tier at $20/month.
- Pro is the high-end individual tier at $200/month.
- Business replaces the older Team naming and is priced per seat.
- Enterprise remains quote-based.
- Usage limits and model access can change with system demand and plan revisions.

### Editorial reality-checks

- ChatGPT is broadly useful, but strong output still depends on prompt quality and human review.
- Search and research features reduce but do not remove the need for source checking.
- Personal plans have different privacy expectations than Business and Enterprise plans.
- Competitors now have clearer niche advantages, so the rewrite should avoid pretending ChatGPT is automatically best at everything.
